This is a website redesign that I worked on for the Mississippi Watershed Management Organization during their 2026 rebrand.

This was a capstone final project for my major, where my whole class worked on different aspects of the rebrand. Two other classmates and I were redesigning two webpages, this one and the one in the collage, as a secondary project. The high-fidelity wireframe for this page was created solely by me in Adobe Illustrator, strictly adhering to the brand kit and checking for WCAG accessibility standards. MWMO requested a simple, navigable design for all levels of interest in Watershed Management, so I chose a modular layout that made content accessible for all audiences. The whole process of creation involved iterative refinement, visual hierarchy, and modular layout design to improve usability.

1. Here is a piece of signage that I created for the Office of Sustainability at UST for their Office Supply Swap.

This signage was created in my Digital Design Studio class in 2026 for an internal employee only office supply swap as they did not have any visual identity yet and so commissioned me and two other members of my class to create a brand identity for them. The only request they made was that it adheres to the Universities identity standards. This signage was requested to help explain how the donations worked. With this in mind, and with an employee only audience, I settled upon making the design simple with few colors and graphics, but still eye catching enough to be noticed. For the process of the design itself, I used Illustrator for the complying of major assets such as the logos, colored borders, and iconography as it gave me a wider range of tools the Canva in which they wished the file to be delivered. In Canva, I merely added the text in the approved font and with the proper weight hierarchy.

This is my favorite work I created for my DIMA 256: Graphic Design class and two of my favorite sodas.

These designs are individual class final projects and not client work, and I was given total control over creating them however I wanted. With that in mind, I wanted to create a product that had a strong visual presentation. My goal was that, even without being able to read the text on the can, shoppers would be able to see the can and recognize the produce it contained from a single glance. To create a product that is recognizable, I focused on what is most distinctive about root beer and ginger beer: their color. Both have strong color associations, so I used only those colors when creating my design in Illustrator and frequently checked that it had the desired effect in the Photoshop mock-up. Since my desired audience was shoppers in a hurry, I kept the design minimal, with graphics and text pared down, and the Root beer's color and beer-mug-like lines conveying what the product is.

Windsong: A Musical Fable is a musical production for which I was commissioned to a create a digital playbill cover.

This is my first-ever professional commission for Annabelle Lee Revak, a London-based composer, lyricist, and playwright, for her original work, Windsong. She requests that it have a visual identity similar to that of fantasy novel covers, but not overly complex or crowded. The process of creating this work was completed start to finish in Adobe Illustrator while listening to the musical performance, ensuring the visual tone matched the musical composition. While Wind Woman was an asset I did not create, I made significant alterations to her by adding to her dress through copying and pasting, as well as by altering and adding anchor points. I also created a left arm for the woman with the line tool and curvature tool. Similarly, the subtitle font was created by freehand use of the eraser tool and changing the color of the lower section.

This is the second album cover in my school career as an alternative cover of Neoni’s 2021 album Wars in A Wonderland.

This was created in 2024 for one of my digital media arts classes, and my favorite song at the time was Wonderland from this album. When designing this, the only restriction my professor gave was that it had to include the album's name and the artist's name, and that it fit visually into the musical from the album. Just like the music, I wanted the album cover to reflect its discord, so that if someone saw it on a music platform, they would know exactly what kind of music it was. While the music itself reflects the negative feelings that came with the COVID quarantine, not Alice in Wonderland, I wanted to combine the two in my design to blend the darkness of one with the nonsense of the other. I started by staging the picture using red and blue studio lights and the random knick-knacks I had. After I had taken the picture, I darkened it on my phone by increasing the shadows and contrast. I then uploaded it to Canva Premium and added the text. I added a glow effect to the font to make it look like neon lights and changed the “a” in all the words to red to create an extra visual distortion.

This photo was taken to show an understanding of German Expressionism for a World Cinema Film Class.

Unlike my other works above, this picture did not involve any software such as Canva or Illustrator and was done entirely on an iPhone. My film professor requested that, after watching Metropolis (1927), each person in the class create an expressionist image that tells a story. For this, I was both the photographer and the reaching hand. To get the shot, I positioned myself and the other subject so that the hallway's natural light cast a double shadow and so that my hand holding the camera could not be seen. I wanted it so that anyone who saw the image would understand the separation and isolation that come with technology, both from the person reaching out and from the secondary, smaller, darker shadow of the person holding the phone, who seems almost to be disappearing into themselves. After I took the photo, I used Apple Photos’ adjust tools to increase the contrast and sharpness of the image as well as darken the shadows for even bolder contrast.
